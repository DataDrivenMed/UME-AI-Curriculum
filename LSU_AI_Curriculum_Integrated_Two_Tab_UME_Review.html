One-Page Executive Summary

The proposed AI-ready physician thread is a longitudinal curriculum overlay. It uses existing medical school courses to teach students how to use AI while preserving the reasoning skills that physicians must retain.

Why longitudinalAI habits are not learned safely in one lecture. Students need repeated, supervised practice across basic science, population health, clinical skills, clerkships, and capstone experiences.
Why not a stand-alone AI courseThe key risks emerge inside actual medical tasks: probability, evidence appraisal, patient summaries, diagnostic reasoning, privacy, and clinical accountability.
Phase 1 pilotStart with POP, biostatistics, epidemiology, CSI, HELP, IPE, and clerkship preparation. Expand only after faculty review and local governance alignment.
Assessment stanceUse short AI-output audits, prompt rewrites, privacy checks, and reasoning explanations. The evidence of learning is judgment, not tool fluency alone.

What This Proposal Does / Does Not Do

This box is included to make UME critique easier and to prevent overclaiming.

Does

  • Supports existing course objectives.
  • Uses AI to protect and assess reasoning.
  • Requires student-first reasoning before AI use.
  • Includes privacy, equity, and governance boundaries.

Does Not

  • Does not turn courses into AI courses.
  • Does not ask students to trust AI.
  • Does not permit PHI in public AI tools.
  • Does not represent an approved LSU SOM curriculum change.

Central Reasoning-Protection Spine

The core educational problem is not whether AI can answer correctly. The problem is whether learners still build the reasoning required to know why an answer is correct.

Six Curriculum Domains

AI literacy is necessary but insufficient. The thread also needs appraisal, privacy, clinical workflow, and governance.

Year 1 to Year 4 Progression

The progression moves from vocabulary and reasoning protection to supervised clinical critique and residency-ready accountability.

Year 1: KnowAI vocabulary, safe-use habits, privacy boundaries, population medicine, and foundational reasoning protection.
Year 2: ApplyEvidence appraisal, prediction, calibration, validation, organ-system cases, and Step-style reasoning protection.
Year 3: PracticeSupervised critique of AI summaries, differentials, patient education, preventive care, and clerkship workflow risks.
Year 4: DemonstrateAI-ready intern judgment, specialty-specific AI critique, governance awareness, downtime, and accountability.

Model Early Pilot: POP / Biostatistics / Epidemiology

Foundations of Population Medicine and Health Systems / POP I / POP II is the best first home because epidemiology and biostatistics already teach uncertainty, denominators, probability rules, risk, predictive value, bias, and evidence.

Teaching point for current AI models

Even when ChatGPT gives the correct answer, the teaching moment remains valid. Students must recognize the reasoning cue before AI does. The goal is not to catch AI being wrong; the goal is to prevent students from outsourcing the setup.

Course director language: "Before we use AI, we answer the reasoning question ourselves. What is the clue? What rule applies? AI can explain the steps, but it should not be the first thinker."

Mini Checklist for Live AI Exercises

Ask students to answer first.
Ask for the clue or reasoning rule.
Run AI second using a fictional or course-provided prompt.
Audit the AI explanation with students.
Name the AI concept explicitly.
Connect to future clinical reasoning.

Examples

Probability rules, relative risk, confidence intervals, sensitivity/specificity, PPV, and NPV.

AI concepts

Prompt engineering, hallucination, human-in-the-loop, false proficiency, RAG limits, data privacy, and guardrails.

Evidence of learning

Students explain the reasoning cue and audit whether the AI answer preserved the reasoning, not just whether the final answer was right.

POP / Biostatistics / Epidemiology Pilot Detail

Use this pilot as the model for other courses: student first, AI second, audit third. The live exercise is short, controlled, and tied to the existing lecture question.

Why this is the best first home

These lectures already teach denominators, probability, uncertainty, association, bias, confidence intervals, screening tests, and predictive value. AI integration therefore reinforces the medical content instead of adding unrelated technology content.

What the course director says

"We are not asking AI for the answer first. We are asking students to identify the clue, rule, formula, or assumption first. Then we use AI to make the reasoning visible and audit whether the explanation was educationally complete."
